Games library

Sessions of 5–15 min. Always end while he's still winning. If he stops taking food → you're done, not him.
FoundationCharge your marker
The marker ("yes!" or a clicker) tells him the exact instant he got it right. Everything else is built on this.
  1. Quiet room. Say "yes!" → treat. No behavior needed.
  2. Repeat ~20 times across 2–3 mini sessions.
  3. Test: say "yes!" when he's looking away. Head whips around = charged.
FoundationHand touch ("touch")
A portable redirect, and it teaches that hands coming toward him = good things. Later this transfers to the sitter.
  1. Palm out at nose height, slightly to the side.
  2. He sniffs/bumps it → "yes!" → treat from the other hand.
  3. Add the word "touch" once he's reliably bumping.
  4. Move the hand around: higher, lower, a step away.
FoundationFind it / scatter
Sniffing actively calms the nervous system. This is also your emergency exit near triggers — toss AWAY from the scary thing and move.
  1. Say "find it!" and scatter 5–6 treats in grass.
  2. Let him forage. That's it. That's the game.
  3. Outside: use it the moment you spot a trigger early, to move him off and down-shift.
FoundationMat = off switch (Relaxation Protocol)
Karen Overall's 15‑day protocol teaches a relaxed STATE, not just a position. A portable safe zone.
  1. Reward any interaction with the mat → then only lying down on it.
  2. Day by day, do small things while he stays settled: count to 10, clap once, step away, jog in place, touch the door.
  3. 15–20 min, 1–2× daily. Don't advance until today's level is boring for him.
  4. New location = restart at day 1. Expect a worse-before-better dip around days 3–7. Normal.
Trigger workLook At That (LAT)
Turns "see a dog" into a game he plays WITH you instead of an emergency. The cornerstone.
  1. Find a distance where he notices the trigger but stays green (still eats).
  2. The INSTANT he glances at it → "yes!" → treat as he turns back to you.
  3. Repeat. Soon: sees trigger → immediately looks at you. That's the win.
  4. Only close distance after 3–5 easy reps in a row. A few steps at a time.
He stares and can't turn back, or won't eat → too close. Add distance, don't repeat the mistake.
Trigger workEngage–Disengage
Level 2 of LAT: he learns to look away from a trigger BY HIMSELF. Self-interruption is incompatible with lunging.
  1. Level 1 = LAT (mark the glance AT the trigger).
  2. Level 2: after a glance, wait 1–5 sec. He looks away on his own → "yes!" → treat.
  3. 3–5 calm reps → take 1–5 steps closer. Any struggle → back up.
Trigger work1‑2‑3 walking game
Pure predictability. A rhythm to walk past spooky things — trucks, people on the sidewalk.
  1. Walk and count "one… two… three" out loud; on "three" a treat appears at your side. Every time.
  2. Drill it in easy places until "three" makes him snap into position.
  3. Use it to move through tight spots before he can build a stare.
Trigger workBAT-style long-line setups
Grisha Stewart's method: on a 15ft line, HE chooses to look, sniff, and walk away from a distant trigger. Choice rebuilds what the prong took away.
  1. Set up far from a stationary trigger (a calm dog behind a fence, a person sitting).
  2. Follow him on a loose line. Let him observe and gather info.
  3. When he disengages on his own — sniffs, turns, walks off — quietly follow. Moving away IS the reward.
  4. Drift closer only as his body stays loose. 10–20 min max.
Ball & playFetch with an off switch
His ball drive is the engine of this whole program. Rules turn arousal into focus.
  1. Hold the ball. Wait for a sit (don't cue it). Sit happens → "yes!" → throw.
  2. He returns → "drop" → the INSTANT he drops, next throw. Dropping restarts the game.
  3. 3–4 throws, then a 30‑sec sniff break ("find it"), then more. Teaches arousal to come DOWN, not just up.
Ball & playTug with rules
  1. Ask for a sit → "get it!" → tug enthusiastically, low and sideways.
  2. Let him WIN often. Winning builds confidence; you are not "showing dominance."
  3. "Out" → go still → he releases → "yes!" → game instantly restarts. The fastest way to teach "out" is that out = more tug.
Ball & playFlirt pole (2–3×/week)
  1. Lure on the ground in wide arcs — never helicoptering at his head height.
  2. Sit/wait → "get it!" → chase → let him catch within a few passes → brief tug → "drop" → reset.
  3. 5–8 minutes on grass, then a sniffy cool-down. End calm.
Ball & playBall-as-search (nosework)
Searching with his nose = independent success = confidence. The ball is allowed to be the target.
  1. Have someone hold him (or use a wait). Hide the ball in easy grass while he watches.
  2. "Search!" → he finds it → party + a game of tug/fetch as the jackpot.
  3. Make hides progressively blind and harder. 10 min, indoors on rainy days too.
ConfidenceMiddle (safe station)
A place to BE when the world gets close: tucked between your legs, facing out, anchored to you.
  1. Lure him between your legs from behind; "yes!" + treat while he's there.
  2. Name it "middle." Feed several treats for staying.
  3. Practice on walks when nothing's happening, so it's available when something is.
ConfidenceParkour-lite (novel stuff)
Paws on a curb, through a gap, around a pole — chosen freely. Novelty he controls becomes novelty he likes.
  1. "Two feet on" a low ledge/log → mark, treat, HIS choice to step up. Never lift or lure-pressure him onto things.
  2. Add: under a bench, through two trash cans, around a tree.
  3. Jump-downs no higher than his shoulder. Check every obstacle is stable first.
ConfidenceMuzzle = peanut butter party
Undo the old muzzle damage and build safety infrastructure for vets and sitters. A basket muzzle, conditioned right, becomes a happy cue.
  1. Weeks 1–2: muzzle appears → treats rain → muzzle disappears. Then treats INSIDE it; he volunteers his nose in. Never push.
  2. Week 3: clip it for 1 second → unclip → jackpot. Build seconds slowly.
  3. Week 4+: peanut butter smeared inside, short wears during play and walks. Off before he fusses.
Any tail tuck or backing away → you went too fast. Drop back two steps. The muzzle must never again appear while he's already stressed.
PeopleIntroducing new people
Strangers don't greet him. They make good things happen from a distance, and HE decides everything.
  1. Brief the person first: no eye contact, no reaching, no talking to him, side-on body.
  2. Person tosses treats AWAY from themselves (or you feed while they're visible).
  3. If he chooses to sniff them, they stay passive. No petting on day one. Ever, if he says no.
  4. Consent test later: chest scratch 3 sec → stop → does he ask for more? His call.
  5. Many short visits beat one long one. Person leaving = treats stop (people predict good things).
Freeze + stare at a person = too close, too soon. Cheerful "find it" away. Never let anyone insist "dogs love me."
DogsIntroducing dogs (neutrality, not friendship)
Goal: a dog who can SEE dogs and shrug. Parallel walking with one calm, stable dog — never dog parks, never nose-to-nose meets.
  1. Recruit ONE rock-solid, indifferent adult dog (a friend's calm dog, or ask a force-free trainer for a "decoy" dog).
  2. Walk same direction, opposite sides of a field. Far enough that Shadow stays green.
  3. Over many sessions, drift closer. Both dogs sniffing ground and loose = good.
  4. Maybe, months in: a brief 3‑second sniff and move on. Maybe not. Both outcomes are success.
Stiff, tail high or tucked, hard stare in EITHER dog → widen the gap. One bad fight now can cost a year.

Read him, head to tail

His body answers before his behavior does. Trust the tail and the food test over everything.

🟢 Green — he can learn

  • Loose, curvy body · weight even
  • Tail neutral or easy wag
  • Soft blinking eyes, relaxed open mouth
  • Takes food, plays, sniffs the ground by choice
  • Glances at things, then moves on

🟡 Amber — lower rungs, add distance

  • Lip licking (no food), yawning (not sleepy)
  • Tail half-tucked · body slowing, stiffening
  • Scanning, ears pinning, head turns away
  • Sudden "fake" ground sniffing, shake-offs
  • Takes food roughly / slower

🔴 Red — over threshold, exit now

  • Refuses food he'd normally take ← the most reliable tell
  • Freeze, hard stare, closed tight mouth
  • Full tail tuck, crouch, whale eye
  • Barking, lunging — or eerie stillness (shutdown)

The ladder he climbs

Kendal Shepherd's ladder of escalation. Interrupt low, and he never needs the top. Punishing rung 8 deletes the warning, not the bite.
  1. Yawning, blinking, nose licking
  2. Turning head away
  3. Turning body away, pawing
  4. Creeping, ears back
  5. Standing crouched, tail tucked
  6. Lying down, leg up
  7. Stiffening, staring
  8. Growling
  9. Snapping
  10. Biting

Real progress vs. shutting down

You learned this the hard way with the old trainer. "Quiet" is not the metric — loose is.

Improving: body gets looser over weeks · tail comes UP · eats in more places · recovers from startles in seconds · checks in with you voluntarily · WANTS to get out of the car · shake-offs after stress · still plays.

Suppressing: still but stiff · "obedient but flat" · tolerates touch with tucked tail · stops trying rather than starts enjoying · avoids places/equipment · stress poop after sessions · won't eat, won't play.

Your half of the leash

Your stress is measurably contagious — long-term cortisol syncs between dogs and owners. So: loose leash, slow exhale, light voice, keep walking. Don't tighten up when you spot a trigger before he does; he reads your hands.

Comforting a scared dog does not reward fear. Fear is an emotion, not a behavior. Be his safe base — that's not babying, that's the mechanism.

The honest map

Non-linear. Adolescence ends ~2.5–3 yrs. Setbacks are weather, not verdicts.

Month 1

Prong gone for good. Marker charged, touch/mat/find-it solid at home, muzzle conditioning started. You can call his color (G/A/R) at a glance. Fewer blowups because YOU manage distance better.

Month 3

LAT works at distance. Eats outside in more places. Threshold distances shrinking. First "sees trigger → looks at you" moments. Starts looking to you when unsure.

Month 6

Noticeably calmer walks. Parallel walks with one stable dog. One trusted new person accepted at home. Begin the sitter ladder below.

Year 1

Mostly neutral to everyday triggers at workable distance. Safe handling by his conditioned caregiver. Possibly first overnight trials. Still selective — that's fine.

Year 2

Mature, stable adult. Calm neutrality + selective sociability is the realistic ceiling — and it is a complete success.

Travel-care ladder

The real goal behind "daycare": someone can safely care for him while you travel. Group daycare is the wrong tool for him — this ladder is the right one. Tap to check off.

About daycare — the straight answer

Group-play daycare would very likely fail him today and could set him back badly — fearful adolescent Malinois are exactly the dogs these environments hurt. A bite there also creates a legal bite history.

What works instead: an in-home sitter he knows well (best), solo boarding with no group play, day boarding with individual walks, a Fear Free–certified sitter/facility, or boarding with a force-free trainer.

Condition the caregiver months before the trip, do a paid local overnight trial first, hand over a written trigger sheet, and have his muzzle conditioning done as insurance. That's how he gets safely cared for while you fly.

When to call in the pros

Veterinary behaviorist (DACVB) — book one regardless; his fear has genetic/early roots and meds may lower the floor so learning can happen. UC Davis behavior telehealth (CA residents): 530‑752‑1393. Dr. Karen Sueda (West LA) covers San Bernardino County.

Force-free coach — CPDT‑KA / CDBC / KPA‑CTP via the CCPDT or IAABC directories. They coach YOU; the leash stays in your hand.

Escalate sooner if: ~3 months of consistent work with little change · he can rarely get green outdoors · any bite · fear spreading to new things.
